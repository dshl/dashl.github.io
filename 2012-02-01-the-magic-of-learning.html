The Magic of Learning

I believe in magic — it's found in the potential inherent in every child.

I woke up punctually at 5:30am. Sliding out from under my cocoon of sheets, I began my morning routine and dressed myself in a tie, collared shirt, and slacks. Accompanied by the house cat, Mr. Whiskers, I would then walk to the refrigerator, ransack last night's leftovers into my backpack, and trek down the stairs into the garage. The stairs creaked. So, I always had to walk slowly, careful not to apply my body weight too heavily on any step. In the garage, I would select one of the many quality bicycles stored there and ride down sloping hills towards work.

I lived in a home–stay blocks away from the Haight–Ashbury district of San Francisco. People say "home is where the heart is" and Ms. Otero, single mother of three, was the heart of this home — a heart that enveloped the entire community. Almost every evening, neighborhood friends would gather in her kitchen to cook, and with a fine bottle of wine we would stay up into the night, laughing and playing games.

I woke up before sunrise today, like everyday, to bike into the San Francisco Day School garage around 6:20am, ride the elevator to the main floor, and greet Ray, the security guard. I loved coming in this early in the morning: the hallways were empty, the copy machine was all mine, and I could revel in the peace and quiet of my own thoughts.

I would walk into my classroom to prepare the white–board. On the far right hand side, I would update a cross–section of the board, divided it into four sections: date, objective, schedule, and homework. After filling in these sections, I would sometimes walk around the classroom with pencil in one hand, my lesson plan on a clipboard in another. I cherished this rehearsal for these empty seats would soon be filled with children and their impressionable minds.

By 7:30am, I was in the faculty lounge for a formal check–in with the other teachers, and by 8am, the day officially began with what we called, "Morning Greeting." As the students arrived at school, we would greet them with a themed dance party.


"Disney Character" theme day.

And in this way, boisterously loud and clothed in Halloween attire, we greeted students to another day at Breakthrough Collaborative: San Francisco — a place where everyone believes in the magic of learning, and its power to change lives.

Consider these facts: [1]

  1. In 2009, only 14% of African-American and 17% of Latino eighth graders achieved proficiency in reading and math, compared to 41% and 45% of their White and Asian classmates.
  2. Only 60% of America's low–income youth can expect to graduate from high school; 1/3 can expect to enroll in college; and merely 1/7 will earn a bachelor's degree.
  3. Low–income youth are 5 times less likely to earn a bachelor's degree by age 24 than a child from a high–resourced family. [2]

The stakes are high. A recent McKinsey & Co report argues that "the quality of an educational system cannot exceed the quality of its teachers." In fact, the quality of a teacher is the single most important variable in the classroom. Even so, the challenge of education transcends the classroom. According to University of Washington economist Dan Goldhaber, about 60% of achievement is explained by non–school factors.

The situation seems dire, but education reform has seen a resurgence. New thought–leaders are inspiring a national conversation. Follow the work of Geoffrey Canada of the holistic charter school network, Harlem Children's Zone, Michelle Rhee of the political advocacy group, StudentsFirst, Sal Khan of Khan Academy, Dave Levin and Michael Feinberg of KIPP public charter schools, among others, and you quickly realize that the common thread among these innovators is a no–excuse policy about the potential inherent in every child.

Throughout my summer at Breakthrough, I taught in a room which was as both a guidance counselor's office and an 8th grade Honor's English classroom during the calendar school year. It was a small, intimate space that barely fit nine desks let alone accommodate nine students, and teacher twice a day for 45 minutes. I was a 5th grade writing teacher. My summer objective was to teach 18 students how to write a personal narrative. I also set out to capture their hearts and arrest their minds with the magic of learning.

The students called me "Mr. Dash" and on my door hung a picture of the character 'Dash' from the Incredibles. Next to the whiteboard, I had a newspaper clipping which read in bold, black letters, "TOTURE IS OVER." Each day as the students prepared to leave my class, I would point to the poster and say, "Torture is over," and they would respond, "No, Mr. Dash, torture has just begun!" It was our inside–joke. Within the first week, I collected 6 assignments without names that promptly found their way into recycling. Those 6 students missed recess to redo the missing assignment. I soon found myself in a trio student's called the "strict teachers" of Breakthrough (and I wore the title as a badge of honor). My students knew that I had their best interest at heart and expected no less than their absolute best. I knew that the world would expect much more from them with much less care and support.

I learned a lot from my students. One student in particular was Kayla. [3] Like many her age, Kayla was a curious extrovert who struggled with a mild case of ADHD. Most days, Kayla would ask so many questions in class that it became a hindrance to my instruction and a discouragement to her when she was ignored. Due to the limited time I had with my students, not all questions could be asked, or answered without wasting valuable time so I decided to implement a teaching tool called "Parking Lots." I placed a sticky note on the upper right hand corner of each class desk. Students could now jot down the questions unaddressed in class. Throughout the class period, I would collect these notes and stick them on the whiteboard where parking lot lanes were drawn for these "sticky questions."

Most students had a hard time understanding my intention with these sticky notes, but that was ok. The notes became another line of communication between my students and me. While some students used the notes to tell me about personal concerns or even make connections to other material, Kayla learned to focus her questions. At the end of class, along with her classwork, she would hand–in her sticky note and I would clear up misunderstandings over lunch. If she didn't have a question, Kayla would turn in sticky note with a smiley face. The summer transformation was magical. Kayla could barely sit through a 45 minute class let alone articulate her thoughts but by summer's end, she among others were writing masterpieces.

I loved what I was doing. I worked on average seventy hours a week, slept late, rose early, and bent over backwards to teach (and re–teach) my lessons. Life had purpose and it was found in these children who taught me to not only believe in magic, but to live under its power every day.
