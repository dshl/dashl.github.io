African Gothic

This time of year, the summer evening settles down
Bathed in a hazy rain come late.
The two guard dogs surprised
Whimper and howl in protest for they prefer to be
Unwashed and unkempt.
I left my letter and let them in
And after we dried off (forfeiting an hour),
The dogs struggled to forget the day and I tired to remember
All that has happened since last I wrote.

The man with the voiceless yell — whoever he was,
And whatever happened to him, he will certainly never read this. He was
In nothing but an outfit of ragged trousers.
A clan of about a half-dozen men drew his body across the road.
His feet were two-cumbered paint stokes.
There isn’t much street light after dark. He was there for his snapshot,
Framed in the headlight beams of our passing vehicle. And then he was
Gone.

Policenmen farm their beats.
Uniformed jackals with their bayonets at the fore. They
are wild
And let none pass
Without the exchange of dirty bills.
Pale tongues salivate, flailing, and crass.
Scavengers at night, officers by day patrolling the shantytowns
Hunched in silhouettes to the darkness.

The cunning bandit who presides over the country leaves no hen untouched.
"Hail, hail the chief!"
The masses cluck in spasms, feathers lifted towards bouts of listless clouds as if to fly
Only it is high noon and the day’s heat beats them backwards In the street,
A large black cock plays
A vicious game of cat and mouse with a crippled frog.  His feet Laced together. His eyes
Burnt shut.

The air is nostalgic somewhere,
Maybe where you are.
At a newspaper stand, a man hurries to make his daily exchange.
"Newspaper please" as he squints to make out the words, "Save Africa."
The man behind the booth collects his reward, "Savings it is?"
"Maybe I’ll retire there. Recolonize a bit of the darkness for myself."

A ban of private militias passed by the other day
Leaving behind a littered trail of grenade shells and of cartridge cases to be collected
Windows shattered, carnage strewn – everywhere
They lay silently wrapped in each other’s arms, a hand
Strokes a face half-hidden in the red dirt – a contorted version of the truth
Am I to be harvester of limbs?
I will not sow – a foot here, a head there – for I do not wish to reap
Instead I walk until I’m tired, close my eyes, and try to weep
The bodies whisper, "It is time to sleep."
In the city, an elevator attendant in one run-down government ministry
Wants a bribe to take me down from the 7th floor to the
6th

At the local zoo, there is a baffled bear with a sore-covered muzzle.
He licks his wounds, ashamed, between short, pockmarked breaths
His heart hammering,
He beats upon his own breast. A boy pounds the ground. His bare feet are silent, skipping in and out. In and out.
He will walk many miles to the Jehovah Jireh Hospital,
To sleep among the bones of his brothers and sisters.
Each night, his dreams are the same:
From the Jungle, the Lord’s Rebel Army draw closer to his village, Demon spawned, they emerge to –
A shadow appears for a moment. An AK–47 strapped to his person.
He has not come to quicken the dry bones of the valley, he is no Elijah.
"Fear not." His name is Gabriel.

The milk–less mother peers into the distance,
She sings a chorus "You will be tall and strong / Rings of gold around your neck / leader of men."
A mirage appears —
A scrofulous eagle perches herself in a dropping-spattered nest.
The mother holds her swollen infant closer to her breast
"… You will live to be old and grey / trace the wisdom in you hands / legacy of no end." When the day breaks, I will tell you the triumphs of
Those we know: The hoarse, red-eyed jungle combatant. The
The Refugee. The Beggar. The Slum Dweller.
And those you have never heard of —
The Nameless.
I will have stories yet to tell, but I am tired.
Tired of stories;
for it is time to live.
